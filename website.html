My Heart, Day to Day

A simple card for living well with Kawasaki disease coronary aneurysms. Built from my own records. Not medical advice.

For me, my mom, my aunt•Updated June 2026

Call 911 if any of these

A clot can form in an aneurysm with no warning, even when I feel fine. Do not drive me. Do not wait it out.

  • Chest pain, pressure or tightness
  • Pain spreading to arm, jaw, neck or back
  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Cold sweat, looking pale
  • Nausea with any of the above
  • Feeling faint or about to pass out
Call 911
Tell the paramedics, every time: "I have Kawasaki disease with coronary artery aneurysms. My biggest is about 7 mm in the left main / front (LAD) artery. Please do not place a regular-sized stent. My heart team is the ACHD clinic at Toronto General."

I keep this on a wallet card and my phone medical ID.

My heart in 30 seconds

  • Had Kawasaki disease as a kid (age 2, again at 7). It left aneurysms (widened spots) in my heart arteries.
  • Biggest: ~7 mm in the left main / front artery, with 50% narrowing. Smaller ones in the right artery. No clot on my 2025 scan.
  • Fitness and pump are strong (stress test low-risk). Two jobs now: stop clots and keep arteries calm.
  • My #1 open question: I am on aspirin alone for clots (couldn't tolerate the stronger blood thinners). Worth pushing my team on alternatives.

Today's checklist

Tick these off each day. Saved on this device only.

Moving safely

Exercise protects me. A few things need a lower gear. Breathe out through every lift, never strain with held breath.

Green: goBrisk walks, easy cycling, swimming, rowing, zone-2 (can still talk).
Yellow: ask firstHIIT/sprints, heavier weights, competitive pickup sports.
Red: avoidContact sports, max lifts / breath-holding strain, all-out racing, sauna → cold-plunge, exercising dehydrated.

Stop and rest for any chest pain, odd breathlessness, dizziness or cold sweat. If it doesn't pass in a minute or two, treat as the emergency list above.

Eating for my heart

Two goals, one plate: LDL as low as possible and reverse the prediabetes (A1c crept to 6.3%).

Best single wins

  • Cut sugar-sweetened drinks first. Biggest blood-sugar lever.
  • Protein + fiber with any carbs. Walk after meals.
Important caution: I have thalassemia trait. Do not start iron pills without a blood test first: excess iron is harmful, and my small red cells are not from low iron.
A simple sample day
  • Breakfast: oats + walnuts + berries, one egg, black coffee/tea.
  • Lunch: greens + quinoa or chickpeas + salmon/sardines + olive oil.
  • Snack: Greek yogurt + fruit, or hummus + veg.
  • Dinner: baked fish + roasted veg + a small portion brown rice/lentils.
  • After dinner: 10–15 min walk.

Keeping track

  • Home: my ACHD clinic, Toronto General. They set my personal schedule.
  • Usually: periodic stress test + CT/MR angiogram of the arteries + yearly bloods (cholesterol, A1c). Higher-risk spots can mean check-ins every 3–6 months.
  • Watch the A1c: it's trending up. Bring it back down with food + walking.
  • Sick with vomiting/diarrhea → hydrate hard; can't keep fluids down for hours → call my team.

Top questions for my cardiologist

1. Clot protection (ask first). I'm on aspirin alone because warfarin and clopidogrel didn't agree with me. Given my ~7 mm left-main aneurysm, is that still enough? Could a different blood thinner (a DOAC like apixaban) or antiplatelet (ticagrelor) work for me? Can we get my Z-score on record?
2. Monitoring. When's my next scan/stress test? What would mean I should come in sooner?
3. Blood sugar. A1c went 5.6 → 6.3 in 9 months. Plan beyond berberine? Should I see a dietitian? (Also: does my thalassemia trait change how A1c is read?)
4. Supplements + meds. Can a pharmacist check berberine with my rosuvastatin, plus my full supplement list?
5. Exercise limits. A heart-rate cap? A safe weight ceiling for lifting? A written plan if symptoms hit?

If you're helping me

For family who aren't medical. You don't need to remember the science. Just these:

  • If he has chest pain, can't breathe, goes pale or sweaty → call 911. Don't wait, don't drive him. Tell them he has Kawasaki disease with heart-artery aneurysms.
  • His daily aspirin is the big one. A gentle "did you take it?" actually helps.
  • Encourage water, a walk after dinner, and no smoking around him.
  • Know where his wallet med card is, and that his heart team is Toronto General ACHD.
  • A good stress test does not mean the clot risk is gone. Both matter. Stay alert even when he seems fine.
